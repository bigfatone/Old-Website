UX CASE STUDY

At a Fintech Startup in Irvine, CA

Background

On June 6th, 2016 I joined a small team of two led by the lead UX designer and project manager of a Financial Technology startup. I came out of Cal State Fullerton's UX program excited to practice what I learned. I looked forward to observe the lead designer with years of experience under his belt. My role was to collaborate with the lead designer and build a plan of deliverables into three major phases: Define, Discover, and Design.

The AI Financial Advisor project was one he had been working on for months before I came on board. He envisioned a financial service that automates on your behalf, always acts in your best interests, and can call in at any time anywhere. Something with current financial advisors was missing. Traditional human advisors charge a hefty premium in exchange for their services. Robo advisors provided automated portfolio services but could not account for every single person's unique life situation. With large advances in technology, the lead designer saw an opportunity to realize his vision. He could leverage the recent boom in artificial intelligence to merge both the availability of a robo advisor with the adaptability of a human advisor together into one AI Financial Advisor.

We needed more information to convince other potential investors, however. Our next step was to conduct market research and get some insights on the current investing world.

Research Findings

My research uncovered signs of financial trouble for the average American household. According to Forbes, two thirds of Americans can't afford a $500 emergency. This means that two thirds of American households are likely to be living paycheck to paycheck. For this lifestyle, any one unforeseen medical bill could easily place a household in a financial crisis.

On top of that, a recent study by the FINRA Foundation also shows that Americans are not confident in their knowledge of financial matters. In the study, 79% of Americans without savings accounts were unable to demonstrate financial literacy.

We knew we set out to help people make smarter decisions with their money, but the severity of the situation still surprised us.

Competitor Analysis

We wanted to see how existing companies were approaching the problem of Americans managing their money. We decided to look at companies that were employing various degrees of automated portfolio management. During competitor analysis, I found that most major companies do not offer a complete suite of services to lower end customers. For example, Vanguard's Personal Advisor Services are only available to account balances over $50,000. Other companies such as Betterment and Wealthfront do not offer much financial advice. After creating the automated portfolio with them, you're on your own. You're in charge of managing your own wealth and your own financial strategies.

User Testing

To confirm the market research from another angle, we decided to conduct user testing and see for ourselves if the studies were accurate. We devoted one Agile sprint on building the survey guide on Typeform, collecting responses, and analyzing our findings.

Our analysis of the 20 participants had sentiments that lined up with Market Research. This suggests that there is indeed validity to the findings.

Personas

We combined information from market research, user testing, and our competitor analysis to create personas. We found that there were four distinct categories of people that think of their money. They were based on their thoughts on retirement, their spending habits, and their level of skepticism with new technology.

When building the personas, we chose a skeleton persona format. With an image left out, it helped keep a neutral unbiased stance when looking at the personas themselves. The four categories are:

  • Primary Persona - one who is willing to try AI, most of income going to expenses

  • Secondary Persona - lower end income, but educated about financial matters

  • Power User - likely already investing and looking to try new avenues of profit

  • The Skeptic - wary of artificial intelligence

We know that many users may fall into many persona categories. We feel that the majority of the user base will fall under the Primary and Secondary with aspects of Skeptic.

Looking Ahead

AI Financial Advisor is still in the early stages of its life. Had I stayed on the project longer, I would have followed up with wireframes and then use it to create an interactive prototype for mobile.

WIREFRAMES

At Cal State Fullerton

Overview

This portfolio piece focuses on a series of assignments based on a cookbook recipes theme. Each week we had to complete an assignment before returning to the next session. Deliverables include a Sitemap, three pages of the Desktop Wireframe, and three pages of the Mobile Wireframe.

My Involvement

I worked on the project alone as the class did not require teams. I figured a cookbook is easy to visualize for reviewing information architecture, so I went with this theme. This way I can make sure I have the concepts down before I confuse the development team when it comes to building the website. The class met from June 4, 2016 to June 18, 2016. Each assignment was due a week after it was assigned.

Sitemap

For the first week we learned about the nuances between sibling pages, child pages, and stacked pages. To avoid confusing the development team, we were to practice what we learned in our own sitemap.

​

For my sitemap I chose to have a flat navigation style like the iOS App Store and stock Music App. This style lends itself to be a database of recipes. I also wanted to have a personalized pantry section. This section works with with recommendations to give you recipes based on available ingredients in the pantry.

​

After peers reviewed my sitemap, I received feedback that it took too many clicks to navigate around. I'll have to find a way to communicate page inter-connectivity without over complicating it.

Desktop Wireframes

For the second week we had to generate wireframes based on our sitemap for the Home screen and two child pages. We were required to annotate on our wireframe and the homework. The instructor rejected the deliverable if we sent in a blank wireframe that lacked annotations. Without annotations there wouldn't be any context on the expected effects of the interactive elements on screen.

​

I decided to make the hero images a carousel of featured recipes to highlight relevant information to visitors. Ideally the carousel will personalize to their search history as well. The search bar is also in plain view, allowing for the user to invoke the search function at any time while visiting the site. ​

This was the week I learned about the fold and how to declare content that is hidden below the fold. During peer review the lack of including the fold confused my classmates and made them think that the page had nothing below the fold.

Mobile Wireframes

For the final week of this class, we had to convert our desktop wireframes to scale well with the various sizes of mobile devices out there. While the sizes do vary wildly between phones, tablets, and phablets, we only had to focus on smartphones for homework. We instead had to consider the different context between accessing a website from a desktop and accessing a website from mobile.

As far as a cookbook goes, I decided that both desktop and mobile users already have a set task in mind. As a result, I kept most of the functionality of the desktop version while hiding a navigation bar under the bars. This worked to reclaim screen space.

Closing Thoughts

During the wireframe weeks, I wondered about the nature of requiring annotations. The annotations themselves aid in communicating expected interactions. I also figured the design team will be present to elaborate on design choices when necessary. I can see complications arising when working from a remote location, however. This suggests that design and development teams may be separate and siloed off from each other. If that's true, then annotations are that much more important to have on hand.
