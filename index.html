UX CASE STUDY

At a Fintech Startup in Irvine, CA

Background

On June 6th, 2016 I joined a small team of three led by the lead UX designer and project manager of a Financial Technology startup. I came out of Cal State Fullerton's UX program excited to practice what I learned. I looked forward to observe the lead designer (named the Boss herein) with years of experience under his belt. My role was to collaborate with the Boss and build a plan of deliverables into three major phases: Define, Discover, and Design.

The AI Financial Advisor project was one he had been working on for months before I came on board. He envisioned a financial service that automates on your behalf, always acts in your best interests, and can call in at any time anywhere. Something with current financial advisors was missing. Traditional human advisors charge a hefty premium in exchange for their services. Robo advisors provided automated portfolio services but could not account for every single person's unique life situation. With large advances in technology, the Boss saw an opportunity to realize his vision. He could leverage the recent boom in artificial intelligence to merge both the availability of a robo advisor with the adaptability of a human advisor together into one AI Financial Advisor.

For my first two weeks on the job I shadowed the Boss while he did his thing. He often showed his style of design through a curated list of apps and websites the Boss had me look at and critique them along with him. I was able to pick up on terms that he dished out as he was jumping from app to app. As a result of this I was able to take in some of his design values.

His design philosophy is:

  • Always account for changing software because technology is advancing at a rapid pace

  • Experiences must be end-to-end, a guided experience the whole way through

  • Keep things as simple and clear as possible

  • Be open to all ideas including the outright impossible ones

To the team, having written core values posted for everybody on the team to see was important. It helped remind us of our product's direction.

Research Findings

In the first month I was to conduct market research. The benefits to start me at the beginning were twofold; it would allow me to ease into the team's workflow, and I would be able to gain an understanding of the business opportunity for the company.

My research uncovered signs of financial trouble for the average American household. According to Forbes, two thirds of Americans can't afford a $500 emergency. This means that two thirds of American households are likely to be living paycheck to paycheck. For this lifestyle, any setback such as a medical emergency derails life goals for years.

On top of that, a recent study by the FINRA Foundation also shows that Americans are not confident in their knowledge of financial matters. In the study, 79% of Americans without savings accounts were unable to demonstrate financial literacy.

We knew we set out to help people make smarter decisions with their money, but the severity of the situation still surprised us.

Competitor Analysis

We wanted to see how existing companies were approaching the problem of Americans managing their money. We decided to look at companies that were employing various degrees of automated portfolio management. During competitor analysis, I found that most major companies do not offer a complete suite of services to lower end customers. For example, Vanguard's Personal Advisor Services are only available to account balances over $50,000. Other companies such as Betterment and Wealthfront do not offer much financial advice. After creating the automated portfolio with them, you're on your own. You're in charge of managing your own wealth and your own financial strategies.

User Testing

To confirm the market research from another angle, we decided to conduct user testing and see for ourselves if the studies were accurate. We spent the first official Agile sprint on building the survey guide on Typeform. We were able to receive 20 complete responses to the survey. We were able to include open-ended qualitative questions in the mix. As a result I felt more confident about having a smaller sample size reveal patterns about their financial matters. If we only used quantitative questions, we would have to increase our sample size before making any correlations in the data.

Our analysis of the 20 participants had sentiments that lined up with Market Research. This suggests that there is indeed validity to the findings. The survey can be viewed here.

Personas

With research from the market and a pool of users, we were now ready to move onto further analyzing the data. To categorize the patterns, I decided to sort commonality among the responses into an Affinity Diagram. This would help remind us about their perception of money and the nature of their responses as well. The data categorized into clusters of responses (shown in white cards). We used our judgment to gauge the sentiment of their responses (shown in red cards).

We used market research, user testing, and our competitor analysis to create personas. We saw from the Affinity Diagram that there were four distinct categories of people that think of their money. They were based on their thoughts on retirement, their spending habits, and their level of skepticism with new technology.

It was around this time when we decided to focus on investing. Americans as well as the respondents in the survey view investing as their method of generating wealth in the long term. When building the personas, we chose a skeleton persona format. With an image left out, it helped keep a neutral unbiased stance when looking at the personas themselves. The four categories are:

  • Primary Persona - one who is willing to try AI, most of income going to expenses

  • Secondary Persona - lower end income, but educated about financial matters

  • Power User - likely already investing and looking to try new avenues of profit

  • The Skeptic - wary of artificial intelligence

We know that many users may fall into many persona categories. We feel that the majority of the user base will fall under the Primary and Secondary with aspects of Skeptic.

User Journeys

To help visualize the user scenarios and communicate them to the Boss, I decided to position them to read from top to bottom. I created user journeys at four distinct points in the interaction and the expected features on that particular page.

White cards label the page and the attached yellow card describes it. The lines describe what the user goes through as they navigate down the chart.

There is an Initial Contact period where the user first comes across the website looking to learn more before getting started. After that, the user gains access to a limited suite of features called Level 0 features. This was a deliberate decision to avoid overloading the user early on. After a while, the user will unlock Level 1 features with light use of the app. As the user gains more experience with the app, the user will unlock Level 2 features.

Looking Ahead

AI Financial Advisor is still in the early stages of its life. Soon I plan to finish the design phase with wireframes and then use it to create an interactive prototype for mobile.

After that, I also plan to write a document for a Usability Testing plan. This is necessary to test the prototype with live users unrelated to the project. It will confirm if the mobile prototype is in fact intuitive before finally committing with developing the app.

  • Date (Duration): June 2016 - September 2016 (3 months)
  • Category: User Research
